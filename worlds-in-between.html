I. Shapes

Shapes are bounded intersections carved out of saturation

A Shape starts with four images chosen from a dense cluster. Those four define a face-plane. Then you either (a) specify a height (up/down distance), or (b) pick a top image and a bottom image to lock the upper and lower planes. The result is a quadrilateral “box” whose faces are populated by nearby images that already exist in the coordinate space.

Select

Pick 4 images (face plane)

Four corners define a local meaning-face. This face is not a collage, it’s a spatial constraint.

Extrude

Choose height or top/bottom

You can extrude by distance, or by anchoring to an image above and below to form upper/lower planes.

Populate

Faces fill with adjacent images

Because the cluster is already settled, the shape’s faces inherit nearby material that matches the face constraints.

Best in ultra-saturated density: Shapes work best where the region is already heavy with meaning, because the face planes have enough nearby images to reveal intersections instead of emptiness.

What a shape “shows”
Overlap zones: images that belong to multiple pulls at once. Faces become readable evidence of intersectionality.
What you can annotate
Notes per image (micro) or a note for the entire shape (macro), explaining what the intersection means.
What the system saves
The selected images, face constraints, height/top-bottom anchors, and the arrangement context that produced them.
II. Shape → Sphere

Curving a box into a world

A Shape is a local patch. A Sphere is the global container. When you map shape faces onto a curved surface, you turn bounded intersections into mini-atlas globes that can live inside the larger Atlas.

Start: faces define local constraints
Map: face planes → sphere surface regions
Preserve: adjacency + overlap signatures
Result: a mini-sphere that behaves like a portable worldlet

Goal: build many mini-spheres that each encode a taste-profile and internal geography, then nest them into larger spheres without losing their identity.

III. Why mini-spheres matter

They become training-grade world units

Each mini-sphere is a compact world with saved constraints. That makes it ideal for learning: you can sample coordinates, measure neighborhoods, and treat each sphere as a consistent environment.

Identity

Style DNA

A sphere carries an aesthetic fingerprint: what “belongs” in its neighborhoods.

Physics

Interior logic

The arrangement rules become learnable constraints, not vague prompts.

Reproducible

Snapshots

Saves preserve the surface conditions, heat, seeds, and dataset, so the world can be rebuilt.

IV. Learning

After enough spheres, the machine learns the hidden interior

Once you’ve built enough spheres, you can train a model to answer a simple question: given a sphere and a coordinate, what should exist there? The model learns this by reading many saved arrangements, surface constraints, and neighborhood structures.

Input

Sphere + surface + coordinate

The model is conditioned on the sphere’s identity, the surface constraints, and the point you’re asking about.

Signal

Heat + intersections

Heat fingerprints and overlap patterns teach what the sphere “cares about” in each region.

Output

Predicted “belongs here” material

A target embedding or signature that matches the local world logic, ready for generation.

Dataset: many spheres + saved snapshots
Train: predict content signature at (x,y,z) given sphere constraints
Use: feed signature into generation to create candidates
Curate: keep what fits, discard the rest
Re-anchor: new material becomes part of the world, strengthening future predictions
V. The World In Between

“BAM”: generate the inside that intersects

The promise is not “make random images.” The promise is: if the surface of a sphere is seeded with meaning, then the interior can be generated as coherent material that intersects those surface constraints. The closer you get to the center, the more the content becomes “close to all things.”

Surface
Surface points (tags, anchors, composites) define the world’s climate: what pulls, what repels, what overlaps.
Interior
The interior is a gradient of intersection. Regions merge, corridors form, and rare hybrids appear where multiple pulls agree.
Center
The center is the “closest-to-all” zone: content that sits near the maximum overlap of the sphere’s major meanings.
Outcome
You can define a world with a small number of surface seeds, and the system grows the interior with candidates that fit, then you keep what you love and the world tightens around it.

World-building loop: seed the surface → generate interior candidates → curate → re-anchor → repeat. Each pass sharpens the sphere into a more precise version of your taste.
