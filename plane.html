Atlas Plane v1 — a board where meaning piles up

Found a Plane on one or more Images and Tags… then watch the archive grow stalagmites.

Atlas Plane v1 is the “board version” of Atlas. You place meaning points on a plane: some are singletons (one image, one tag, one tag-cluster), and some are composites (multiple images + tags + clusters bundled into one point). Each point has heat. Heat is gravity. The archive aggregates underneath, building towers where the pull is strongest.

How the plane is founded What a point can be Why stalagmites happen
Plane: a 2D field of points
Points: images + tags + tag clusters (single or composite)
Heat multiplies influence
→ archive images aggregate under points, forming stalagmites (vertical piles)

Focal founding

The plane can “spawn” from a single seed point (one image or one tag) and the archive arranges outward and downward from it.

Composite points

Points can represent bundles: image+image+tag-cluster, or any mix. One handle, one heat slider, big effect.

Stalagmites

Instead of folders, the archive piles up. Where influence is strong, the tower gets tall.

Move points

Drag a point across the board and the aggregate reshapes. Rearrangement is the product, not a side effect.

1. Founding the plane

The board can be born from a single seed

If you add nothing else, a single founding point can define the plane. The archive arranges outward (across the field) and downward (into height) from that focal origin.

Seed

Single Image founding

Pick one image as the “origin.” Similar images cluster nearby; dissimilar ones drift outward or form their own towers.

Seed

Single Tag founding

Start from one concept. The plane forms as a gradient of that meaning, with piles where subthemes become dense.

Seed

Multi-seed founding

Found the plane on multiple inputs (images/tags/clusters). The field becomes a negotiated geography between pulls.

Default behavior
No extra points added? The archive still organizes. The plane “fills itself” from the founding seed(s).
Then you add anchors
Add points later (spread out) to carve territories, create bridges, and force splits or merges of piles.
Seed: Image heat 1.0
Tag Cluster heat 0.8
Composite: Img+Img+Tag heat 1.3

for each image I in archive:
  score(I, S) → initial placement on plane
  height(I) increases where local density + pull is strongest
→ result: a board with emergent piles even before you add more points
2. Points

A point can be a singleton or a composite

Plane v1’s core move: treat “a bundle of meaning” as one draggable point. A point can be one thing, or a composite made of multiple images and tag structures.

Singleton

Tag

A single term. Clean influence. Great for carving broad territories.

Singleton

Image

A single anchor image. It’s an aesthetic lighthouse: style, vibe, composition, mood.

Singleton

Tag Cluster

A group of tags treated as one point. A cluster lets you say “this whole concept space” with one handle.

Composite Points (the real power)
v

A composite point is a single draggable node made from multiple inputs. Think of it as a “mini-foundation”: a blended magnet you can place anywhere, then tune with one heat slider.

Examples (still readable, not gimmicky)

  • Image + Image
  • Image + Image + Image
  • Tag Cluster + Tag Cluster + Tag Cluster
  • Image + Tag Cluster
  • Image + Image + Tag Cluster
  • Image + Tag Cluster + Tag + Image (cleanest verbal “sales handle”)
Define a point P as:
P = { images: [i1..ik], tags: [t1..tn], tagClusters: [c1..cm] }
Influence(P, I) = heat(P) × blend(sim(I, images), sim(I, tags), sim(I, clusters))
→ one point, one gravity, one rearrangement handle
Key rule
A point can be one thing or any combination of things. The system treats it as one attractor with one heat slider and one position.
Why composites matter
They let you say “this vibe” without forcing you to write 20 tags or find one perfect image. You just bundle meaning.
3. Heat

Heat is the multiplier that reshapes the board

Heat changes how hard a point pulls. It’s the simplest control that produces dramatic reorganizations without changing the data.

Neutral

Equal heat = honest arrangement

When heats are similar, piles form where the archive naturally clusters. It’s the most faithful “truth map.”

Bias

Higher heat = stronger gravity

Turn a point hot and it absorbs mass: more images aggregate under it, towers grow taller, neighbors thin out.

Dynamics

Heat creates “territory theft”

A hot point expands its basin. Routes and borders change. The board breathes when you adjust sliders.

Heat multiplies a point’s pull:
low heat → subtle guidance
high heat → dominant attractor (towers grow)

Move a point + change heat = you can reshape the entire map without re-tagging the archive.
4. Aggregation

Why “stalagmites” are the right metaphor

On the Plane, images don’t just sit near points. They pile. The pile is the visual summary of density: the more images the point captures, the taller the tower.

Horizontal
Where it belongs (placement on the plane): proximity to meaning points and composite anchors.
Vertical
How much belongs there (height): density of captured images under that pull signature.
Emergence
Towers form “cities” and “ridges.” Bridges appear where pulls overlap.
Plane coordinate (x,y) says: nearest meaning neighborhood
Tower height (z) says: how many images “agree” with that neighborhood
→ browsing becomes: walk the field, climb the towers
What you do

Exploration feels like moving through terrain

You click a tower to open a local gallery. You drag points to change borders. You heat a point to make the world care more about it. It’s geography for meaning.

Minimal v1 controls

  • Found the plane: start with one or multiple seed points.
  • Add points: singleton or composite meaning points.
  • Move points: drag across the plane to reshape regions.
  • Heat slider: change tower growth and territorial pull.
  • Click-to-browse: open the images “inside” a local tower.
Explainability (why is this image here?)
v

Every image can expose a “pull signature”: which points attracted it, and how heat weighted the decision. That makes the terrain trustworthy instead of magical.

5. Pitch note

Why the explanation stays restrained

Note: I didn’t want to make this look gimmicky just to sell the concept, so I kept the system described here at a clean cutoff. The real scope of my vision easily expands to “as much as you want” combinations, complexity be damned. But for explaining the idea clearly, I found the simplest verbal handle is a composite point like Image + Tag Cluster + Tag + Image. It communicates the core mechanic without drowning the reader in permutations.
