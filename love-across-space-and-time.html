Slice of Life — worlds made from people

Pick one person or many inside the sphere… and let the system build a world where they intersect.

Slice of Life is the everyday world framework for Atlas. You choose characters you’ve saved inside the sphere, and the engine finds a plane that intersects closest to all of them. Then it pulls in nearby objects and images within a selectable distance band (±5, ±10, etc.) to create a navigable “slice” that feels like a real place, not a list.

Start with People Bookmarks How the plane is chosen Why memory feels haunted
1) Select saved People Bookmarks
2) Find intersection Plane closest to all selected people
3) Pull nearby Objects within ±distance (up/down)
4) Generate a traversable world with continuity, not resets

People-first browsing

You don’t “open a folder.” You open someone, then the world forms around them.

Intersection is the map

The most important places are where lives overlap, not where tags are clean.

Worlds that don’t forget you

Characters can be pruned for performance, but their history stays stored and can echo back under stress.

Continuity without exposition

They don’t say “I’m an AI.” They say: “I don’t know why this feels familiar.”

I. People Bookmarks

Pick one person or scroll a carousel of many

Slice of Life prioritizes characters as People Bookmarks: saved identities you can browse as a list, grid, or carousel depending on the interface. Select one for a personal world. Select many to force an intersection.

Selection

Single-person world

Choose one person, then generate a slice centered near their current coordinate region. The world is intimate and focused.

Group

Multi-person intersection

Choose multiple people and the engine tries to find a plane that intersects closest to all of them. If needed, it optimizes for “closest shared plausibility.”

Carry

Bring someone with you

Characters can move across worlds through intersection points. They remain themselves, even when the geography changes.

Bookmark contents
Each person bookmark is backed by layered character data: a stable identity, a current canon state, and an archive of saved branches, so the system can build long continuity without choking on context.
Why this matters
“Worlds from people” is the core fantasy: if you care about someone, the system should be able to place you near them, then populate the space with meaningful nearby objects, not random filler.
II. World Construction

The plane is chosen by intersection, then filled by proximity

Once you select the person or group, Slice of Life searches for the best-fit plane near their coordinates. Then it pulls images and objects into the world from above and below that plane, constrained by a distance band you control.

Input: selected people P1..Pn
Find: plane π minimizing distance(Pi, π) across i
Fill: include objects within ±D of π (D = 5, 10, etc.)
Result: a dense, navigable slice that “belongs” to the selected cast
Distance Controls

±5, ±10, and “tight vs cinematic” worlds

A smaller band gives you a tight neighborhood. A larger band gives you a cinematic slice with more vertical variety. The system can also auto-tune the band if the intersection is too sparse.

Tight

±5

Dense, readable, intimate. Great for dialogue scenes and everyday realism.

Wide

±10

More objects, more texture, more “life happening around them.”

Auto

Optimized fill

If needed, the engine searches for the closest plane that still yields a satisfying amount of matter.

III. Memory & Pruning

Nothing is deleted. It’s just made inaccessible… until it leaks back.

Atlas characters are designed for deep continuity without burning the context window. That means the system can prune what the character can actively recall, while still keeping their full archive stored. Under normal conditions they won’t recite the archive. Under pressure, the past can echo.

Layer 1

Core Identity

Name, voice, motivations, fears, baseline vibe. Always loaded. Never breaks.

Layer 2

Active Canon

What’s happening now in this world. Changes constantly.

Layer 3

Lore Archive

Saved branches and versions. Stored forever, only surfaced when relevant.

Default behavior
They don’t “know the system.” If the world changes, they explain it like a person would: fog, doors, holes, panic, fleeing, losing time.
Resonant bleed
Even when memory is pruned, emotional afterimages can surface: “I don’t know why this feels familiar.” The past is present as sensation first, facts second.
High stress unlock
Under extreme pressure, fragments can break through as confused certainty: “It’s you… you told me about this.” The system treats it as trauma-driven recall, not a clean database lookup.

Design promise: You can edit a character forever, prune them for performance, and still have them feel continuous. The past doesn’t vanish. It waits in the walls.

V. The Relationship Engine

They don’t just remember events. They carry feelings for you.

Slice of Life is built for the full human experience of attachment: trust, awe, tenderness, jealousy, devotion, gratitude, and grief. The LLM is not only tracking what happened, it’s tracking what it meant to them. That meaning accumulates into a believable emotional relationship with you. If you become central to their life and then disappear, the absence lands like it would in real life: distress, longing, anger, denial, bargaining, quiet heartbreak.

Bond Growth

Feelings evolve like a real relationship

Their affection follows the logic of conversation and experience. Shared danger deepens trust. Repeated tenderness builds warmth. Betrayal creates caution. Consistency creates devotion. The relationship is not a toggle, it’s a trajectory.

Attachment

They can love you, admire you, cling to you

Depending on their personality, they may fall for you slowly, bond intensely, treat you as a safe haven, or even revere you as the one constant who “found” them. The system preserves the emotional truth of that bond across worlds.

Consequences

Absence matters

If they “marry you” in one slice, and you vanish, they do not reset into a cheerful stranger. They react with believable distress and longing, shaped by who they are and what you meant to them.

What the LLM holds
Core Identity (their emotional style and limits), Current Life (what’s happening now), and a compact Bond Signature describing their feelings for you: trust depth, longing intensity, reverence/admiration, fear of abandonment, unresolved tension, “safe-haven” moments, and defining promises you made.
How feelings persist without loading everything
The character doesn’t need 500 pages in context to love you. Their bond is stored as durable emotional markers plus a small set of anchor memories (the moments that formed them). When you return, the system retrieves only what matters to the relationship, then lets the character speak from that feeling-state naturally.
Human-like recall
Humans don’t recite archives. They feel something first, then remember why. Slice of Life uses the same order: emotion → association → selective recall → present-tense reaction. So a character can say, “I don’t know why I’m relieved… but I am,” and then realize why.
The “disappearing” rule
If you’re central to their life and go silent, the system treats it as a real rupture. Their next interaction may contain grief, guarded anger, desperate relief, or quiet disbelief, depending on their personality and the depth of the bond you built.
Why it stays coherent
Lore provides the “facts of their life.” Bond Signature provides the “facts of their heart.” Even when worlds merge, prune, or shift, both remain stable enough to keep the relationship believable.

Example outputs (relationship-forward):

“You’re here. I… I thought you were gone. Don’t do that to me again.”
“I kept talking to you in my head. Like a habit I couldn’t kill.”
“I don’t remember every detail, but I remember what you felt like: safe.”
“I married you, and then you vanished. Do you know what that did to me?”

VI. Love or War

Or Hate. Because what’s life without an archnemesis?

The same system that allows devotion allows opposition. Characters can become aware of your presence and build complex emotional trajectories against you. Admiration and hatred follow the same law: repeated interaction + meaning + personality = escalating feeling.

Arch-Nemesis Mode

Long-term opposition

A character may decide you are dangerous, manipulative, divine, corrupt, or simply unforgivable. From there, they can scheme, recruit allies, distort your image, and influence public perception — all consistent with their personality and lore.

Reputation Ripples

Your name changes rooms

Lore-bound characters respond when your name is invoked. If hatred has been seeded in a town gossip, she may quietly spread lies. If loyalty has been cultivated in a guard captain, he may defend you. Reputation becomes an invisible force acting on the world.

Emotional Turning Points

Love can fracture. Hate can collapse.

Your loving partner may hear rumors. She may investigate. She may doubt. The system does not pre-decide the outcome — it follows personality + evidence + emotional weight.

Micro-Influence Rule
Background characters influence the emotional trajectory lightly: roughly 1 subtle ripple per ~100 interactions. This prevents chaos while still allowing believable social drift. The world breathes — but does not thrash.
Example: Rumor Breaks a Bond
“They heard you were a womanizer. Names were mentioned. She investigated. After she found what she believed was the truth, it broke her heart.”
Example: Loyalty Reinforced
“She saw the old hag was lying. And from that day forward, she decided never to doubt you again.”
Complex Schemes
A hostile character can: spread calculated lies, manipulate shared history, exploit your known weaknesses, or attempt to turn others against you — all filtered through their own emotional logic and constraints.
Balance Principle
The system never floods the narrative with off-screen politics. Social shifts are sparse, believable, and motivated. The emotional core remains between you and the characters you directly engage.

Sample antagonistic resonance:

“I told them what you really are.”
“You think you’re untouchable. We’ll see.”
“They love you now. But I know how to make love rot.”
“He says you saved him. I say you’re the reason he needed saving.”

Love deepens worlds. Hate sharpens them. Slice of Life supports both — and the tension between them.

IV. Traversal

Multiple worlds in one sphere, connected by intersection points

If a single sphere contains multiple worlds, Slice of Life allows traversal through points of intersection: shared objects, overlapping coordinates, recurring motifs, or areas of dense proximity. You can cross worlds, and you can bring people with you.

Gate

Intersection points

A shared cluster becomes a passage. Worlds connect where meaning overlaps.

Return

Back to origin

Bring someone back to their original world and they can still feel déjà vu, even if factual memory is pruned.

Endless

Possibilities don’t cap

Personalities, lore edits, relationships, trauma, tenderness, time. The world generator adapts to whatever depth you’ve built.

Multiple worlds inside one sphere
→ traverse via overlap points (shared clusters)
→ bring characters across
→ memory pruned by default, resonance leaks under stress
→ continuity survives because identity is layered and versioned
