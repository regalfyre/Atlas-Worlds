Atlas Sphere v1 — A bounded world for meaning

Put Tags and Singular Anchor Images on a Sphere… then let the archive settle inside.

Atlas Sphere v1 is the simplest “globe version” of Atlas. Instead of organizing images into folders, you define a surface of meaning using single tags and single anchor images. The system then places every image inside the sphere at the coordinate where it best fits those surface forces.

Single points, big effect

1. Surface inputs

What you place on the sphere

In Atlas Sphere v1, the surface is the control layer. You place two kinds of singular “meaning points”:

Surface Point

Tag Node

A single tag becomes a single point on the sphere. Its position matters because distance creates influence.

Surface Point

Anchor Image Node

A single anchor image becomes a single point. It functions like a “style magnet” or “aesthetic lighthouse.”

Weight

Heat per node

Heat is strength. It changes how much mass a tag or anchor can pull toward itself from the interior.

Surface layout meaning
Nodes that are closer on the sphere “blend” more often. Nodes that are far apart create distinct territories. Your surface layout is literally the world’s climate system.
What v1 avoids
No complicated multi-tag polygons. No folder trees. No forcing users to pre-decide categories. Just: place points, tune heat, observe the world settle.
2. Interior placement

How the contents of the sphere rearrange

Every image in your archive is assigned a coordinate inside the sphere. That coordinate is not arbitrary: it is the location where the image best fits the combined pull of all surface nodes.

Inputs per image
The system compares an image to each surface tag and each surface anchor image, then combines those signals with per-node heat weights.
Output
A coordinate in interior space (a stable point you can revisit) plus a ranked explanation of which nodes pulled it there.
Rearrangement
When you move a surface node or change heat, images shift. Clusters can tighten, split, drift, or merge. The globe is a living cartography.
For each image I:
1) compare I → each Tag Node
2) compare I → each Anchor Node
3) multiply by Heat weights
4) choose the interior coordinate that best matches the combined surface pull
→ result: a stable placement + an explainable “why”
What you see

Clusters become geography

The interior fills with structures you can navigate: dense “cities” of similar meaning, “bridges” where concepts overlap, and sparse “deserts” where rare images live.

Atlas Sphere v1 visual language

  • Cluster: a dense pocket of nearby images
  • Region: multiple clusters under a similar set of surface pulls
  • Route: a gradient path between clusters
  • Anchor drift: a region reshaped after placing or moving an anchor image
3. Heat

Heat changes what the sphere “cares about”

With equal heat, Atlas Sphere v1 shows the most neutral, faithful arrangement: images land where the natural fit is strongest. Heat is how you bias the world intentionally.

Default

Equal heat = neutral truth

All surface nodes exert equal influence. The interior placement reflects the most “honest” approximation to your tags and anchors.

Increase

Higher heat = stronger gravity

Raising heat on a tag pulls more images toward that concept, even if those images also relate to neighboring nodes. The cluster absorbs mass.

Tradeoff

Heat “steals” from neighbors

A hot node expands its basin of attraction. This can thin nearby clusters and reshape routes. It’s a deliberate distortion for usefulness.

Heat acts like a multiplier on influence:
low heat → a node becomes subtle background guidance
high heat → a node becomes a dominant attractor (more images cluster near it)

Result: you can make the sphere “care more” about one concept without rewriting your whole tagging system.
4. Coordinate space

Every image becomes a coordinate you can return to

Atlas Sphere v1 treats an image as a point in meaning-space. That point is stable for a given surface layout. If the surface changes, the point moves, but it remains trackable through its identity and explanation.

Coordinate ID
A compact identifier representing the interior coordinate plus the surface configuration that produced it.
Relational descriptor
A readable breakdown of how strongly the image aligns with each surface tag and anchor, including heat context.
Bookmarkability
You can bookmark a cluster region or a single image coordinate, then return later even after exploring elsewhere.
5. What “paired to coordinate space” means

It’s not “stored here.” It’s “belongs here.”

In normal systems, an image is “in a folder.” In Atlas Sphere v1, an image is “at a coordinate.” That coordinate is the system’s best mathematical claim about where the image belongs under your surface forces.

What you can do with that

  • Jump directly to a coordinate to “teleport” to its neighborhood.
  • Compare two image coordinates to see how similar their pull signatures are.
  • Track drift over time as you move tags or adjust heat.
  • Build named regions and routes (your own atlas) and share them.
6. Exploration

How it feels to use Atlas Sphere v1

The experience is “world navigation,” not “search.” You move from place to place, open neighborhoods, and bookmark regions so you never get lost.

Click

Click a cluster, open a neighborhood

Click a dense pocket in the sphere to open a local view of the nearest images to that coordinate. It’s browsing by proximity of meaning.

Bookmarks

Save regions as “places”

Bookmark clusters with notes so you can return later. The system treats meaning like geography: routes, regions, and landmarks.

Re-shape

Move surface nodes, watch the globe breathe

Slide a tag around the surface, or nudge an anchor image. The interior rearranges and shows you what shifts, splits, or merges.

Minimal v1 controls (simple on purpose)

  • Surface placement: drag tag nodes and anchor image nodes around the sphere.
  • Heat slider: per node, raise/lower influence.
  • Click-to-browse: click a point inside, open a neighborhood gallery.
  • Bookmark + annotate: save a cluster as a named location with notes.
